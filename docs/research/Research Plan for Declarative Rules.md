# **Research Plan: Curating and Structuring Training Material for Adapter A (Declarative State Rules and Textbook Theory)**

## **Executive Summary of the Research Initiative**

The integration of declarative knowledge—specifically rigid state rules, textbook theory, and deterministic physical constraints—into the parameter space of Large Language Models (LLMs) represents one of the most profound architectural challenges in contemporary artificial intelligence. While extensive pretraining over massive corpora effectively encodes procedural knowledge, linguistic heuristics, and associative pattern matching, it frequently fails to instill robust, generalizable declarative facts and deterministic state-tracking capabilities. Probabilistic models inherently struggle with closed-system logic, leading to hallucinations in grid navigation, physics commonsense violations, and the degradation of absolute factual recall.  
This research plan outlines an exhaustive, end-to-end methodology for identifying, curating, structuring, and deploying training material for a specialized Low-Rank Adaptation (LoRA) module, designated as "Adapter A." Adapter A is engineered to imbue foundation models with strict adherence to "Textbook Theory," which is defined in this context as declarative state rules, spatial coordinates, deterministic physical logic, and closed-universe game mechanics. The objective is to decouple procedural reasoning from declarative factual recall, ensuring the model can operate as a deterministic engine within strictly defined parameters.  
To achieve this, the strategy leverages the Apple Silicon MLX framework (mlx-lm) to execute computationally efficient fine-tuning protocols on consumer and workstation hardware. By utilizing the specific architectural affordances of modern foundation models—particularly the highly optimized Gemma 4 family, including its Edge (E2B, E4B) and Mixture-of-Experts (26B A4B) variants—this plan details a pathway to translate abstract coordinate grids, physical boundaries, and textbook axioms into optimized textual datasets. The research design specifies dataset topologies, JSONL formatting protocols, tokenization strategies, and a novel "memorize-then-generalize" training paradigm designed to mitigate the rote memorization pitfalls, catastrophic forgetting, and reversal curses commonly associated with factual knowledge injection.

## **The Cognitive Architecture of Declarative Knowledge Injection**

To successfully curate training material for Adapter A, it is paramount to first delineate the boundary between procedural and declarative knowledge within the latent semantic space of language models. Understanding this taxonomy informs the specific pedagogical structures required for the dataset.

### **The Taxonomy of Parametric Knowledge: Procedural versus Declarative**

Unconscious, procedural knowledge acquired during the pretraining phase drives the foundational reasoning patterns observed in LLMs.3 Pretraining instills a vast, multi-dimensional matrix of grammatical syntax, pattern recognition, and associative logic. This enables the model to execute procedural tasks, such as step-by-step mathematical computation, basic logical deduction, and syntactic formulation.4 Recent literature on the cognitive taxonomy of language models demonstrates that reasoning performance primarily arises from these procedural patterns, indicating that parameters encode generalizable solution strategies as unconscious skills rather than explicit factual recall.3  
Conversely, declarative knowledge encompasses specific, addressable factual representations, absolute state rules, coordinate matrices, and explicit textbook axioms.3 While a model might procedurally understand how to formulate a sentence describing movement, the declarative knowledge dictates the absolute boundaries of that movement within a specific coordinate space. Conditional knowledge acts as the intermediary, governing when and why the model should apply specific declarative rules or procedural heuristics based on the task environment.6  
When researchers attempt to inject declarative knowledge into a neural network via Supervised Fine-Tuning (SFT), models inherently struggle to balance the new facts against their preexisting probabilistic weights. Foundational models tend to excel at memory-driven factual recall but experience significant performance degradation when tasked with complex reasoning or inductive logic over newly introduced, out-of-domain factual constraints.5 If an LLM is fine-tuned on arbitrary identifiers or highly specific rigid coordinates without semantic anchoring, it typically resorts to rote memorization of token sequences because the novel symbols lack participation within the model's preexisting semantic embedding space.7  
The following table categorizes the distinct knowledge paradigms targeted by the broader foundation model versus the specialized parameters of Adapter A:

| Knowledge Paradigm | Characteristics within Language Models | Primary Acquisition Mechanism | Application Focus for Adapter A |
| :---- | :---- | :---- | :---- |
| **Procedural Knowledge** | Unconscious skill execution; syntax formulation; generalizable associative reasoning strategies and logical deduction.3 | Pretraining (Next-token prediction over massive, diverse corpora).4 | Provides the foundational logic to process input sentences and output formatted JSON/text structures. |
| **Declarative Knowledge** | Explicit, addressable facts; rigid textbook axioms; state definitions; spatial coordinates; factual recall.3 | Supervised Fine-Tuning (SFT); Explicit facts injected via text formats.9 | Encoding specific physical rules, grid coordinate limitations, and rigid operational laws for closed systems. |
| **Conditional Knowledge** | Metacognitive monitoring; knowing when, where, and why to apply specific declarative rules or procedural heuristics.5 | Reinforcement Learning (RL), preference tuning (DPO/PPO/GRPO).3 | Triggering strict, deterministic rule-adherence exclusively when queried about simulated environments or puzzles. |

### **The Interference Hypothesis and the Memorization Dilemma**

A critical impediment to sourcing and structuring training data for Adapter A is the tendency for SFT to induce either catastrophic forgetting or mere rote memorization without genuine systemic comprehension.7 When models learn rigid textbook facts, they often suffer from the "reversal curse"—the systemic inability to infer bidirectional logic, such as understanding that if action X leads to state Y, state Y can be reversed by the inverse of action X.7  
Furthermore, the "Interference Hypothesis" must be carefully managed. Empirical evaluations of maritime autonomous decision support agents, such as the 14B-parameter MARTIAN model deployed on NVIDIA Jetson edge hardware, illustrate this phenomenon vividly. When researchers utilized a verified dataset of 21,800 Chain-of-Thought (CoT) instruction-response pairs to align general linguistic capabilities with maritime procedural logic, they found a critical trade-off. While Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG) significantly enhanced pure factual recall in knowledge-intensive domains (boosting total accuracy from 73.23% to 81.16%), it concurrently introduced semantic noise that degraded performance in pure logic reasoning tasks, such as collision regulation maneuvering (which decreased from 78.53% to 77.36%).9  
This data supports a decoupled cognitive design principle: separating procedural reflexes from declarative knowledge.9 Adapter A must function purely as the declarative repository. To ensure the newly injected facts endure, research utilizing probing datasets like "Outlandish" suggests that there is a "sweet spot" in the spectrum of fact novelty; facts that gently conflict with common knowledge are remembered for tens of thousands of training steps, whereas purely arbitrary or entirely nonsensical data is rapidly forgotten.12 Consequently, the training data for Adapter A must be structured using lexicalized symbols (words with semantic meaning) rather than purely arbitrary coordinate hashes to allow the new rules to participate in the model's preexisting embedding geometry, supporting generalization to unseen pairs.7

## **Conceptualizing "Textbook Theory" as Deterministic State Rules**

For the specific operational requirements of Adapter A, "Textbook Theory" is not conceptualized merely as historical trivia or scientific encyclopedias. Instead, it is operationalized as the absolute, deterministic rules governing closed systems, spatial coordinates, and physical properties. This requires extracting physical commonsense, spatial boundaries, and kinematics from traditional simulation software, game engines, and logic matrices, and translating them into purely textual formats that a language model can parse and internalize.

### **Text World Models and Physical Commonsense**

Language models lack inherent spatial awareness; spatial reasoning does not emerge spontaneously in LLMs the way linguistic fluency does, requiring models to cobble together experiences and strategies.16 Standard foundation models exhibit profound physical commonsense violations when analyzing deterministic systems—such as permitting objects to pass through walls, failing to update bounding boxes correctly, or hallucinating grid coordinate transitions based on linguistic probability rather than physical reality.17  
To overcome this, the dataset must construct Text World Models (TWMs). TWMs are transition models over textual states that, given a state and a candidate action, predict the resulting environment entirely in text.19 Physical worlds are governed by embodied regularities (game physics, mechanics, optics), whereas digital worlds are governed by computational regularities (APIs, GUIs).19 Adapter A targets the textual representation of these physical and spatial regularities.  
Data curated from environments like "PhysGame" and "PhysInstruct" will serve as foundational material. PhysGame comprises extensive video-based and textual benchmarks evaluating physical commonsense violations across domains like mechanics, kinematics, optics, and material properties.17 To train the adapter, we will ingest data from the PhysInstruct dataset, which features over 140,057 QA pairs facilitating physical commonsense learning, alongside the PhysDPO preference optimization dataset, which contains 34,358 training pairs specifically highlighting dis-preferred responses generated via temporal, spatial, and meta-information hacking.17 By exposing the model to explicit textual rules governing these domains, we replace probabilistic hallucination with declarative certainty.

### **Grid Spatial Understanding and Coordinate Matrices**

The foundational layer of textbook spatial theory relies on absolute coordinate tracking. Research utilizing the Grid Spatial Understanding (GSU) benchmark highlights that while LLMs grasp basic grid concepts, they struggle severely with embodied frames of reference.20 Specifically, models fail to distinguish between Cardinal settings (where spatial directions are fixed to the grid axes, meaning moving forward always increases the Y value) and Egocentric settings (where directions rotate based on the agent's current heading).20 Furthermore, models consistently fail at identifying 3D structures from 1D coordinate lists.20  
To inject this declarative knowledge, the training material must explicitly define these frames of reference using text-only grid representations. The dataset will include zero-shot, text-only spatial reasoning tasks focusing on navigation, object localization, and structure composition, stripping away visual perception to force the model to rely entirely on textual coordinate tracking.20

### **The Sokoban Paradigm: Declarative Game Rules**

Puzzle environments provide the optimal closed-system domains for extracting explicit textbook theory rules. The classic game Sokoban is frequently utilized in AI research because it isolates long-horizon planning and strict state persistence.24 Generative AI classically fails at Sokoban, frequently generating solutions that cause boxes to pass through solid walls, pushing crates into dead ends, and hallucinating invalid coordinate transitions because the LLM is predicting the next statistically likely word rather than executing a physics check.18 Furthermore, LLM performance degrades severely when more than 25 moves are required, indicating a fundamental constraint on forward planning capacity without explicit state-transition verification.24  
To train Adapter A, the research plan dictates the translation of Sokoban and similar GridWorld rules into explicit textual declarations using variable assignments.25 By stripping symbols of their semantic associations, computation shifts from pattern matching to logical inference.25 The dataset will explicitly format the deterministic rules textually:

* **Axiom 1 (Impassability):** Walls (\#) are completely blocked. The player cannot move into a wall. Boxes cannot be pushed into a wall. Treat walls as impassable boundaries.26  
* **Axiom 2 (Player Movement):** The player (@) can move up, down, left, or right into an empty space ( ) or a target (.). Diagonal movement is invalid.26  
* **Axiom 3 (Kinematics):** If the player moves into a box ($), the box is pushed one step in the exact vector of the player's movement, strictly conditional upon the target coordinate containing an empty space. A box cannot be pushed into a wall or another box.26

The dataset will feature thousands of parsed string representations of these grids, presenting the board state as a matrix of characters alongside explicit coordinate mappings (e.g., Player position: (7,7), box positions: (3,3), (5,5)). The required completion will be the step-by-step state update: \<U\> Move the player from (7,7) to (6,7), box positions are kept as (3,3), (5,5).26

## **Data Sourcing and Curation Strategy**

The acquisition of high-quality, dense training data for Adapter A requires a multi-tiered sourcing approach, prioritizing zero-shot correctness, step-by-step state tracking, and absolute absence of semantic contradiction. The research material will be curated from established repositories and explicitly reformatted.

### **Identifying Raw Material Sources**

1. **Custom GridWorld and MDP Environments:** We will source state-transition data from customized Markov Decision Process (MDP) environments. Repositories such as prasenjit52282/GridWorld and MATLAB-based custom GridWorld generators provide the programmatic foundations to extract thousands of discrete-time stochastic control process logs.27 By running automated agents through generic two-dimensional grids featuring obstacles, we will generate exhaustive JSONL records of valid and invalid ![][image1] state transitions.28 Datasets such as "GridWorld-10x10: Navigation Transitions for Reinforcement Learning" available via Kaggle will be parsed into conversational prompt-completion pairs.29  
2. **Sokoban and Planning Domain Definition Language (PDDL):** The dataset will extract textual representations of Sokoban puzzles.26 We will source environments that utilize domain-specific languages (DSL) based on YAML or PDDL to define environment mechanics.24 Equipping the dataset with PDDL parsing and validation tools allows the language model to learn the formal syntactical structures of declarative logic, mitigating inherent architectural limitations in forward planning.24 We will also incorporate advanced variants, such as Sokoban configurations featuring warp gates and teleportation portals, to teach the model non-linear coordinate jumps and subtle logic nuances (e.g., portals deactivating when blocked by boxes).31  
3. **Natural Language Geocoding Databases:** To ensure the declarative rules extend to real-world spatial reasoning, the dataset will ingest material from systems like Element84's Natural Language Geocoding v0.1.0 database.32 This database encompasses over 5 million places, prioritizing comprehensive polygon coverage over simple point coordinates, and maintaining strict hierarchical context across continents, countries, states, and local areas.32 This teaches the model explicit geospatial declarative structures and bounding box logic.  
4. **Code Execution and Rule Abstraction:** We will incorporate tasks from datasets like the Mostly Basic Programming Problems (MBPP), explicitly focusing on execution codes that act as logic grading systems.33 By showing the model the raw Python code used to execute a physics engine, we force the adapter to learn the underlying programmatic rules governing the textual output.

### **Imperative versus Declarative Data Structuring**

The curation strategy requires the data to be partitioned into two distinct pedagogical formats to maximize the adapter's capacity for scene synthesis and layout generation.34

* **Declarative Formatting:** The LLM is provided with the overarching constraints and boundaries in a single block. The dataset prompt specifies the layout constraints explicitly (e.g., "The board is a ![][image2] matrix. The player is at ![][image3]. The goal is ![][image4]. State the overarching rules for traversal.").28 This mirrors systems like LayoutGPT which explicitly specify the coordinates of objects.34  
* **Imperative Formatting:** The LLM is provided with explicit, iterative step-by-step instructions for constructing or navigating the scene. This approach generates explicit instructions for constructing scenes by iteratively placing objects with relative positioning, mirroring the "Scene Language" approach to layout generation.34

By supplying the model with an apples-to-apples comparison of declarative and imperative approaches during fine-tuning, the adapter learns both the absolute rules of the environment and the step-by-step execution pathway required to navigate it.34

## **The Pedagogical Framework: "Memorize-Then-Generalize"**

The central obstacle in training Adapter A is the conflict between rote memorization and zero-shot generalization. Direct factual injection via SFT often leads to overfitting; the model simply parrots the training data but fails to apply the physics rules to novel coordinate grids. Conversely, attempting to teach pure reasoning without rigid guardrails causes the model to hallucinate the facts themselves.7  
Furthermore, research indicates that the choice of fine-tuning datasets significantly impacts downstream factual accuracy based on the popularity of the facts within the original pretraining corpus. Fine-tuning on examples corresponding to the most popular facts results in significant improvements, but this difference is amplified negatively when injecting highly obscure or out-of-domain knowledge, leading to a degradation in factuality.35  
To bypass these limitations and ensure robust knowledge injection that resists the reversal curse, this research plan dictates the implementation of the newly established "memorize-then-generalize" paradigm.36

### **Phase 1: Verbatim Rote Memorization**

The first phase of the training curriculum intentionally induces rote memorization of the underlying state rules and textbook relations.36 The curated dataset is specifically constructed to strip away natural language variance. Instead of conversational prose, the subject-object associations (or coordinate-state associations) are presented using rigid, non-semantic key tokens.  
For instance, the model is fed thousands of compressed associative arrays mapping positions to states using an arbitrary key token structure such as A \[X\] B: State\_Player\_\[7,7\] State\_Player\_\[1, 2\].26  
During this phase, we leverage datasets containing hundreds of facts per relation (e.g., 100 deterministic grid outcomes per movement vector). The LLM is fine-tuned for a high number of iterations specifically to force these unadorned representations deep into the parametric memory. At this stage, the model acts purely as a compressed database of deterministic outcomes, encoding the "Textbook Theory" without attempting to understand it.

### **Phase 2: Semantic Generalization**

Once the raw spatial grids, coordinate geometries, and declarative physics are heavily memorized, the second training phase involves light, targeted fine-tuning using a much smaller set of semantically rich prompts.36 The dataset for Phase 2 introduces conversational and varied linguistic queries designed to trigger the previously memorized data arrays.  
A Phase 2 training example reformats the data into a standard chat template:  
{"messages":}  
Extensive empirical evaluations across multiple models (including the Qwen 2.5 and Llama 3.2 families) demonstrate that this two-stage approach allows foundation models to cross-reference the rote-memorized rules with their pre-trained procedural language skills. The model successfully reinterprets the rigid key tokens from Phase 1 and generalizes the state transitions to entirely unseen semantic scenarios and novel prompts, overcoming the traditional fragility of factual SFT.36  
The structure of the dataset curriculum is outlined below:

| Training Phase | Dataset Composition | Objective | Output Format |
| :---- | :---- | :---- | :---- |
| **Phase 1: Rote Memorization** | High-volume, highly rigid matrices; abstract key tokens; non-semantic associations (100+ facts per relation).37 | Force parameters to store specific physical laws and coordinate limits.36 | Raw text completions or heavily structured JSON.37 |
| **Phase 2: Semantic Generalization** | Low-volume, diverse linguistic framing; natural language queries (approx. 10 English prompts per relation).37 | Teach the model to route natural language questions to the explicitly memorized declarative rules.37 | Multi-turn conversational Chat templates.38 |

## **Implementation Strategy: The Apple MLX-LM Ecosystem**

To construct and fine-tune Adapter A efficiently, the data pipeline and training loop will be built entirely upon the Apple MLX framework, specifically utilizing the mlx-lm and mlx-lm-lora ecosystem.38 Designed by Apple Research, MLX provides a unified memory architecture ideal for handling the massive context lengths required for multi-step coordinate tracking and physics simulations.39  
The mlx-lm package is natively integrated with the Hugging Face Hub, enabling researchers to seamlessly download, quantize, and execute parameter-efficient fine-tuning on consumer hardware, such as the MacBook Pro M-series, in a matter of minutes.40

### **Framework Configuration and Command-Line Tooling**

For Adapter A, the primary execution pipeline will utilize Quantized LoRA (QLoRA). This technique loads the base foundation model in 4-bit quantized precision during both the forward and backward passes, updating only a small subset of high-precision adapter weights, dramatically reducing the memory overhead while preserving the model's procedural intelligence.38  
The command-line execution via the mlx\_lm.lora (or mlx\_lm\_lora.train) module requires strict adherence to system paths, custom dataset keys, and memory management arguments to process the dense textbook datasets 38:

* \--train: Activates the fine-tuning training mode, looking for the default train.jsonl files in the specified data directory.38  
* \--fine-tune-type lora: Explicitly defines the adaptation methodology, though DoRA (Weight-Decomposed Low-Rank Adaptation) and full-weight fine-tuning are also supported if computational resources permit.38  
* \--batch-size 4 or lower: Given the dense, token-heavy nature of spatial coordinate grids, memory constraints often require scaling the mini-batch size down to ![][image5] or ![][image6] to prevent out-of-memory errors.38  
* \--grad-accumulation-steps \<N\>: To compensate for the low batch size, gradients are accumulated over multiple steps before updating the parameters, effectively simulating a larger batch size for stable training.38  
* \--num-layers 16: Instructs the framework to apply the LoRA adapters exclusively to the final 16 transformer layers. This is a critical configuration; it heavily focuses the declarative updates on the model's deeper, semantic output layers, preserving the foundational linguistic syntax embedded in the earlier layers.38  
* \--grad-checkpoint: Enables gradient checkpointing, which trades compute time for reduced memory usage by recomputing intermediate activations during the backward pass—a necessity when dealing with elongated sequence lengths typical of textual grid states.38

Once training is complete, the mlx\_lm.fuse script will be utilized to fuse the low-rank adapters back into the base model, exporting the final architecture into GGUF formats or uploading directly to the Hugging Face model registry.38

### **JSONL Data Formatting and Key Mapping**

For mlx-lm to properly parse the curated textbook and state-rule data, the structural formatting of the training material must be mathematically exact. The data loader expects local datasets to be strictly formatted as train.jsonl, valid.jsonl, and test.jsonl.38 Crucially, every single training example must exist on a single line; multi-line JSON structures will cause the loader to fail.38  
The dataset will be synthesized into four primary structural modalities supported by mlx-lm, based on the specific type of textbook theory being injected:

| MLX-LM Format Type | JSON Key Structure and Syntax | Primary Pedagogical Application for Adapter A |
| :---- | :---- | :---- |
| **Chat Format** | {"messages": \[{"role": "system", "content": "..."}, {"role": "user", "content": "..."}, {"role": "assistant", "content": "..."}\]} 38 | Multi-turn spatial reasoning; simulating step-by-step game environments (e.g., Sokoban move histories) and Phase 2 semantic generalization.38 |
| **Completions Format** | {"prompt": "...", "completion": "..."} 38 | Single-turn declarative fact injection; mapping specific coordinates to static, deterministic outcomes.38 |
| **Text Format** | {"text": "..."} 38 | Raw, unstructured textbook corpora; continuous rule lists, physical logic axioms, and raw PDDL files.38 |
| **Tools Format** | {"messages": \[...\], "tools": \[{"type": "function", "function": {"name": "...", "description": "..."}}\]} 38 | Training the model to recognize when to execute programmatic validation scripts to verify a coordinate change before answering.38 |

If datasets are pulled directly from Hugging Face repositories (e.g., using \--data "Goekdeniz-Guelmez/..."), custom field names must be explicitly mapped in the YAML configuration or via CLI flags, such as using \--text-feature "content" or \--prompt-feature "question" to align the dataset schemas with MLX's expectations.38

## **Target Foundation Architecture: The Gemma 4 Paradigm**

The curated datasets, formatting configurations, and MLX-LM commands are explicitly tailored to target the Gemma 4 model architecture.46 Released by Google, Gemma 4 is a state-of-the-art family of open-weights models highly optimized for local inference and agentic workflows.46  
The models are available in variants designed specifically for edge deployment (E2B and E4B, designating "Effective 2 Billion" and "Effective 4 Billion" parameters) up to workstation-class models (such as the 31B Dense model and the 26B A4B Mixture-of-Experts).47 The 26B A4B model is particularly relevant for Adapter A; it contains 128 total experts but only activates 8 experts (totaling roughly 4B active parameters) during inference, providing the vast parametric memory capacity of a 26B model with the blazing inference speed of a 4B model.46

### **Architectural Advantages for Declarative Rules**

The Gemma 4 architecture introduces several structural elements that are critical to processing the dense, deterministic rule sets required by this research plan:

1. **Massive Vocabulary Size:** With a vocabulary size of 262,144 tokens, Gemma 4 is significantly less likely to fragment complex coordinates, physical variables, and numerical representations into arbitrary sub-word tokens.46 This vast vocabulary improves the stability of spatial reasoning, as entire grid matrices can be tokenized more efficiently.  
2. **Per-Layer Embeddings (PLE):** In the highly efficient E2B and E4B models, rather than scaling the total network depth or width, each individual decoder layer possesses its own small embedding lookup table for every token. These tables are large but are used exclusively for quick lookups, maximizing parameter efficiency during highly specific reasoning tasks.50  
3. **Expansive Context Windows:** The architecture natively supports context lengths of up to 128K tokens for the edge models, and an astonishing 256K tokens for the 12B, 26B, and 31B variants.46 This is an absolute requirement when passing elongated, multi-step game state histories, massive spatial bounding box lists, or dense textbook corpora in a single prompt for zero-shot evaluation.48  
4. **Native Multimodality and Dynamic Vision Resolution:** While this research plan prioritizes textual declarative rules, Gemma 4 natively supports interleaved multimodal input.46 It utilizes a dynamic vision resolution budget, allowing image token counts to scale from 70 up to 1120 tokens per image.49 This ensures future scalability if the textbook theory requires ingestion of visual diagrams or visual grid representations.

### **Strict Chat Template Formats and Control Tokens**

When utilizing the chat format in mlx-lm, the framework automatically applies the target model's native Jinja chat template via Hugging Face's tokenizer definitions (chat\_template.jinja).38 Gemma 4 utilizes a highly specific and strict control token vocabulary that must not be corrupted during the dataset synthesis phase.54  
Unlike earlier models that used generic \<|im\_start|\> or \`\` tags 38, the Gemma 4 tokenizer relies on specialized turn-based delimiters:

* **System Instructions:** Initiated exclusively via the system string.54  
* **User Turn:** Marked at the beginning by \<|turn\>user and closed with the \<turn|\> token.54  
* **Model Response:** Marked by \<|turn\>model and closed with the \<turn|\> token.54

Furthermore, if the dataset incorporates functional tool-calling (e.g., teaching the model to route a coordinate check to an external Python script before finalizing its answer), the data must utilize the precise \<|tool\_call\> and \<tool\_call|\> delimiters, along with string escaping specific to the Gemma 4 parser.54 Any disruption, syntax error, or legacy tag usage in this templating format will significantly degrade the efficacy of Adapter A, leading to severe tokenization misalignment and hallucinated structural artifacts during inference.58

### **The Thinking Channel: Implicit vs. Explicit Conditioning**

Gemma 4 models natively feature built-in reasoning modes, allowing the model to "think" step-by-step before answering.46 This internal state is managed via specific control tokens (\<|channel\> and \<channel|\>) representing the Chain-of-Thought (CoT) processing, which is typically hidden from the end-user.54 Larger variants, such as the 26B and 31B models, may occasionally generate a thought channel even when the thinking mode is explicitly turned off via API configurations.54  
For Adapter A, managing this CoT capability is crucial. While procedural logic benefits immensely from explicit "horizontal" CoT generation, declarative knowledge retrieval often requires immediate factual recall without lengthy exposition.5 Research into "implicit answer conditioning" suggests that models can perform reasoning "vertically" among the hidden states in different layers without explicitly producing words one-by-one.60  
When configuring the MLX-LM server or running inference scripts, researchers can pass "chat\_template\_kwargs": {"enable\_thinking": false} to disable the explicit thought channel.59 This forces the model to rely purely on the declarative facts injected into the LoRA weights rather than attempting to procedurally deduce the answer on the fly, providing a cleaner evaluation of the adapter's factual retention.5

## **Research Protocol and Validation Metrics**

To empirically verify that Adapter A has successfully internalized the textbook theory and deterministic state rules, a rigorous post-training evaluation framework will be executed utilizing both native mlx\_lm validation tools and established benchmarking datasets.

### **Native Perplexity Evaluation**

A systematically held-out dataset of textbook rules and coordinate changes (structured as test.jsonl) will be evaluated utilizing the \--test command within the mlx\_lm.lora module.38 This calculates the test perplexity (PPL), measuring the model's raw next-token prediction certainty over completely unseen coordinate configurations.38 A significantly lowered PPL on the test set confirms that the model has internalized the underlying logic of the spatial grids rather than merely overfitting to the training samples.

### **Cross-Domain Spatial Generalization**

The fine-tuned model will be subjected to the rigorous Grid Spatial Understanding (GSU) tasks.20 The evaluation will specifically test the model across the three core variants: navigation, object localization, and structure composition.20 Success will be measured by the model's ability to maintain a perfectly stable, error-free trajectory through a textual grid matrix without hallucinatory coordinate jumps, explicitly testing its mastery of both Cardinal and Egocentric spatial frames of reference.20  
Furthermore, the model's forward planning capacity will be evaluated utilizing the Sokoban benchmark. We will specifically analyze degradation in planning performance when the problem requires more than 25 moves to reach a solution. Overcoming the inherent ![][image12] move degradation barrier will indicate a fundamental leap in the model's capacity for explicit state-transition verification via the injected adapter weights.24

### **Evaluating "No Chain of Thought" Recall**

To ensure the knowledge is genuinely declarative rather than a byproduct of procedural hacking, specific evaluations will be conducted in a "No Chain of Thought" setting.3 The model will be tasked with providing immediate factual answers (e.g., identifying the absolute coordinate outcome of a collision) in a single turn. By evaluating Sycophancy and Fact Retrieval without intermediate CoT steps, we can confirm that the data resides in the model's rapid-access declarative memory.33

### **Measuring Reversal Curse Resistance and Semantic Drift**

Finally, the model will be evaluated using the DUAL (Dual Unlearning Evaluation across Training Stages) benchmark concepts to ensure the facts are robust.12 The model will be queried with inverted semantic prompts (e.g., instead of asking "What action causes the transition from 6,7 to 7,7?", we will ask "If the transition was 6,7 to 7,7, what was the preceding action?").7 Confirming bidirectional factual awareness proves the efficacy of the "memorize-then-generalize" training paradigm.37  
Concurrently, a parallel evaluation on general conversational benchmarks will be executed. Because injecting declarative knowledge via RAG or SFT can introduce semantic noise that disrupts the unconscious procedural logic embedded during pretraining (as observed in the MARTIAN maritime studies), this step ensures that the addition of Adapter A does not critically degrade the model's baseline linguistic fluidity and general-purpose intelligence.9

## **Synthesized Conclusions and Strategic Pathway**

The curation, structuring, and integration of training material for Adapter A represents a highly specialized intersection of knowledge representation, cognitive taxonomy, and parameter-efficient fine-tuning. By systematically stripping spatial coordinates, physical boundaries, and deterministic closed-system logic of their visual ambiguity and reformatting them into purely textual, JSONL-compliant declarations, we construct a mathematically rigorous representation of "Textbook Theory."  
Leveraging the Apple mlx-lm ecosystem guarantees optimal hardware and memory management through quantized adapters, gradient accumulation, and mathematically precise masked-prompt cross-entropy calculation, ensuring that gradient updates are expended solely on declarative facts rather than the conversational prompt.38 Utilizing the Gemma 4 architecture provides the necessary infrastructural support—via its massive 256K context windows, 262K vocabulary size, and Per-Layer Embedding efficiency—to handle elongated, dense coordinate histories without token fragmentation.46  
Most critically, by structuring the data pipeline around the bifurcated "memorize-then-generalize" paradigm, this research plan mitigates the traditional limitations of factual fine-tuning, directly combating the reversal curse and the degradation of general reasoning.36 The resulting adapter will successfully bridge the cognitive gap between procedural linguistic fluency and rigid, declarative physical logic, enabling the underlying foundation model to navigate closed-system state rules with deterministic accuracy.
